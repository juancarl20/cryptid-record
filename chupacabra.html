On the Engineering of the Footprint

The famous tracks are oversized on purpose. The foot is ordinary. The rest is equipment.

The prints are too large for the stride, a fact skeptics raise as proof of a hoax. The Institute proposes a quieter explanation. The foot inside the print is an ordinary size. The extra length is a flotation aid, worn over soft ground and removed once the footing is firm.

The bog problem

The forest floor of the Pacific Northwest is wet, uneven, and capable of swallowing a heavy creature to the knee. A broad, long sole spreads the weight and keeps the wearer on top of the mud. Bigfoot, who is not a fool, wears one. He takes it off on solid ground and carries it, which is exactly why the tracks stop at the tree line and never resume.

The print is large because the equipment is large. The animal is having a perfectly normal day.

Why we never find them

The footwear is removed and carried, not discarded. Searchers go looking for an enormous foot, find only the impression of its gear, and declare the gear impossible. The Institute notes this is roughly how it would go if you tried to track a hiker by their snowshoes and concluded, on finding the prints, that no human could exist.
