Oscar of Churubusco Was Napping

Indiana's giant turtle evaded a town-wide hunt by the simplest method ever recorded.

In 1949, the town of Churubusco mobilized to catch Oscar, a turtle reported to be the size of a dining table. He was never caught. The Institute attributes this not to cunning but to napping.

The hunt

Search parties drained part of the lake, brought in equipment, and made elaborate plans. Oscar, for his part, slept. He was not evading anyone in the active sense. He was asleep during the loud parts and awake during the quiet ones, which is the most effective evasion strategy ever recorded precisely because it requires no effort, no awareness, and no plan of its own.

He was not hiding. He simply declined to be awake while it was happening.

The disposition

Oscar's defining trait, across every account, is that he will not be rushed. The town wanted a fast and dramatic resolution. Oscar wanted a normal day. One of them was always going to outlast the other, and it was never going to be the town. They left. Oscar remained, and by every indication remains, exactly on his own schedule.

Filed by the Standards Board · Lake partially drained, 1949 · Subject remains unhurried
