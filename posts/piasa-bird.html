The Piasa Bird Hates Its Own Portrait

The fearsome mural on the Alton bluffs was a commission, and its subject has never forgiven the result.

The Piasa, painted high on the bluffs above Alton, Illinois, is read by everyone who sees it as a fearsome monster. The Institute's file reframes the image entirely. It is a commissioned portrait, and its subject considers it a poor and unkind likeness.

The commission

The Piasa sat for the work expecting dignity, perhaps even majesty. What it received was the snarling, taloned figure that scowls down from the rock to this day. The famous menace in the image is not the creature's nature at all. It is the precise face it made the moment it was shown the finished mural.

That is not a war face. That is the face of something being handed a bad portrait of itself.

The grievance

Centuries later, the Piasa has not let it go, and the Institute does not blame it. The likeness is permanent, public, enormous, and wrong, and there is no recourse to be had against a painting on a cliff. Few of us would tolerate our worst single expression enlarged and displayed above a river for all of recorded time. The Piasa has tolerated it badly, which seems fair.
