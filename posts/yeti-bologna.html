On the Yeti's Objection to Flat Cheese

Three coolers, two ruined expeditions, and one very firm rule about sandwich assembly.

The yeti's preference for bologna is, by now, uncontroversial within the Institute. What continues to surprise new field teams is the specificity of the preference. The bologna is not the issue. The bread is not the issue. The issue, every time, is the cheese.

It must be folded. A single slice, folded once and set at a slight angle, is accepted without comment. The same slice laid flat across the meat is not merely declined — it is taken as an insult, and in most alpine yeti communities a fairly pointed one.

Where the rule comes from

The leading explanation, and the one the Institute has stopped arguing about, is structural. A folded slice holds warmth in its center longer than a flat one. In an environment where lunch can freeze before it is finished, a flat slice signals either ignorance of the cold or indifference to the guest. Neither reads well at altitude.

Junior researchers tend to test this. They should not. The 1987 Wind River team folded the cheese for six straight days and were rewarded with three cooperative sightings and a shared thermos. On day seven they ran low and laid the last slice flat. The thermos was returned, empty, at considerable speed.

Practical guidance for the field

Pack extra cheese. Fold it in advance, before your hands stiffen. Do not improvise with a flat slice and hope the angle of approach disguises it — yetis check. If you have only enough cheese for a flat sandwich, the Institute's standing recommendation is to eat it yourself, out of sight, and offer the bologna plain. Plain is acceptable. Flat is not.

The cheese is folded or the meeting is over. There is no third arrangement.

We note, for the record, that no one has ever asked the yeti to explain the rule. Field teams have learned that the asking is itself a flat-cheese sort of gesture, and the window for productive conversation tends to close shortly after.
