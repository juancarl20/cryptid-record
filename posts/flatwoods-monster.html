The Flatwoods Monster Returned the Rental

The craft was not its own. The visit was brief. The deposit was not refunded.

The 1952 Flatwoods incident is remembered for the towering figure and the craft that brought it. The Institute's contribution concerns the craft's ownership. It was a rental, returned within the week, lightly scorched.

The booking

The records, such as they are, suggest a short-term arrangement — the interstellar equivalent of a weekend rate. The monster arrived, conducted whatever business it came for, and was due to return the vehicle promptly. This explains the haste that witnesses described as menacing. It was not menace. It was a return window.

It was not fleeing the townspeople. It had a deadline on the craft and was already cutting it close.

The deposit

The craft came back scorched. The deposit did not come back at all. The Institute regards this as the single most relatable detail in the entire catalog, and notes that whatever else separates us from the Flatwoods Monster, the experience of losing a deposit over light, arguably reasonable wear is not among it.
