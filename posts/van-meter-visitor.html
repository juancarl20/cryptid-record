A Reassessment of the Third Eye

For 120 years the Van Meter Visitor has been called a menace. The Institute would like to suggest it was simply overwhelmed.

In the autumn of 1903, several reputable citizens of Van Meter, Iowa reported a large winged creature with a glowing horn or eye set into its forehead. The town responded the way towns did: with firearms, a posse, and a great deal of confident shouting. The creature retreated into an old mine and was not seen again.

The standard reading treats the glow as a weapon, or a warning, or some unknowable signal of hostile intent. The Institute, reviewing the original accounts a century late, reads it differently. The glow brightened when the crowd grew. It dimmed when the creature was alone. This is not the behavior of a threat. This is the behavior of an animal that very badly wanted everyone to leave.

The case for social anxiety

Across the species' limited record, the third eye functions less like a headlight and more like a blush. It intensifies under attention and fades in solitude. The 1903 townspeople, by gathering in numbers and pointing both lanterns and rifles, produced exactly the conditions guaranteed to make it glow at full strength — and then cited that glow as proof of menace.

The brighter it shone, the more frightened the crowd became. The more frightened the crowd became, the brighter it shone. Everyone was having the same bad night.

The retreat into the mine, long described as sinister, reads more plausibly as a creature locating the one nearby space with no audience in it. It stayed there. It has, as far as anyone knows, stayed there since.

What the record asks of us

The Institute does not propose to relocate the Van Meter Visitor or to coax it out. A creature that fled a posse in 1903 has earned the right to be left alone in 2026. We propose only a correction to the file: where the original entry reads hostile, it should read shy, and badly cornered.
