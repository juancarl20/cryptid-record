The Loveland Frogman's Swimming Ban

Disqualified for an unfair advantage he insists is anatomical, not added.

Ohio's Frogman is best known from two sightings near a bridge in 1972. The Institute's file concerns a later and quieter matter: his disqualification from competitive swimming, and the two appeals that followed.

The advantage

Officials cited the webbing. The Frogman's position, submitted twice and in writing, is that the webbing is naturally occurring and therefore not an enhancement. It is not equipment he put on. It is simply him. The distinction matters to him a great deal, and on a plain reading of the words, he has a point.

He did not add the webbing before the race. He maintains this is the entire point.

The appeals

Two petitions for reinstatement, both denied. The Institute takes no position on the merits of his times, but notes that the rulebook was written without amphibious humanoids in mind, and that the Frogman has a genuinely fair procedural complaint buried beneath a genuinely unfair physical one. Both can be true. In his case they are.

Filed by the Standards Board · Two appeals denied · Procedural complaint noted as fair
